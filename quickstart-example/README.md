# An Example Family Archive - Built by Hand

This is a small, **entirely fictional** family archive (the Riveras and Lees). It
was put together the way you'd do it yourself: in a plain text editor, by hand, with
**no tools and no machine-generated ID codes** - just folders, text files, and
`[[wikilinks]]`. It's the filled-in twin of the blank `quickstart-template`.

None of these people are real.

## What to look at

- **`people/`** - one folder per ancestral couple, numbered 002-014. Start at
  **`002 - Sam & Jordan Rivera`** (the couple this archive is built around) and
  climb up through the parents (004, 006) and grandparents (008-014).
- **`people/connections/`** - relatives who aren't direct ancestors, like
  `Aunt Carol`.
- **`people/stubs/`** - loose threads, like `the Garcia family`, waiting to be placed.
- **`sources/`** - the evidence. Three records show the range: a birth certificate, a
  newspaper obituary, and a census page. Open `Sam Rivera birth certificate.md` to see
  how a document is recorded and linked to the people it names.
- **`places/`, `notes/`, `inbox/`** - the place list, open research questions, and a
  not-yet-filed item (`grandmas photo box`).

## Sourced facts vs. family stories

Most of what's written in these profiles is **unsourced** - memories and color, like
who coached Little League or who made the best tamales. That's welcome: a `## Notes`
section is for the story. A few facts, though, point at a **source** in double
brackets - Sam's birth date cites `[[Sam Rivera birth certificate]]`, Robert's Korea
service cites `[[Robert Rivera obituary 2008]]`, and James's railroad job cites
`[[1950 census - Brooks household]]`. The rule of thumb the archive uses: anything you
want treated as *proven* should point at evidence; everything else reads as story.

## How the linking works

Open any person and you'll see names in **double brackets**, like
`[[Michael Rivera]]`. Each one points to another file. In an editor that
understands wikilinks (such as **Obsidian**), these become clickable and can be
drawn as a family graph. That web of links - people to parents, sources to people -
*is* the family tree.

Notice there are **no ID codes** anywhere (no `P-...` or `S-...`). A real archive
can have a tool add those later for durability, but they're never required - a
human-built archive linked purely by name is completely valid.

## A note on names (especially women who changed them)

You'll notice the women are filed under their **birth name**, not their married
name: Jordan's file is `Jordan Lee`, not `Jordan Rivera`; Sam's grandmother is
`Maria Garcia`, not `Maria Rivera`. The married name isn't lost - it's recorded at
the top of each file as a `name_variants` alias, and it's all over the stories.

Why birth name? Because that's the name a person appears under in their **earliest**
records - birth, baptism, the census as a child - which is exactly where genealogy
research gets hard. Filing everyone under the name they kept for life (their birth
name) keeps them findable. So when you link a married woman, link her birth name:
`[[Maria Garcia]]`. It does take a little discipline by hand; the optional tools, if
you ever use them, make the aliases resolve automatically so either name works.

(Men who never changed their name, and anyone unmarried like `Aunt Carol`, are
simply filed under the one name they had.)
